Part I

Reporting Discretion and the Quality of Bank Disclosure

On measuring the quality of bank disclosure...



Bank disclosure is informative insofar as it can distinguish between financially weak and healthy institutions.

$$P(\text{Distress}) = f(\text{Fundamentals})$$

If: $$\text{Fundamentals} \sim \text{Disclosure}$$

Then: $$P(\text{Distress}) \sim f(\text{Disclosure})$$

Ability of bank disclosure to explain bank distress varies substantially across countries...

Can the variation in the observed classification performance be attributed to the banks' exercise of accounting discretion?

Testing the link between reporting discretion and the quality of bank disclosure...



Main Hypothesis

Banks residing in jurisdictions with lax disclosure rules or non-stringent enforcement of the rules by the regulators, exhibit lower quality disclosure.

This should be reflected in the reduced ability of their disclosure to anticipate financial distress.

Measuring bank discretion...

For each index, higher values of the index correspond to either better disclosure standards, or a more stringent implementation of the standards by the regulator.

Cross-sectional test



Looking at the banks in a given country-year:

Is the information content of the accounting signal greater in countries with better disclosure?

Information content \(\approx\) magnitude of the loading on an accounting variable in a distress-prediction specification that controls for country-year fixed effects

Loading expected to be greater (in absolute magnitude) in countries with more reporting discretion.

$$Pr(D_{ict} = 1) = \text{Logit}(\alpha_{ct} + \theta_1 * x_{ict} + \theta_2 * R_{ct} * x_{ict} + \epsilon_{ict} )$$

Time series test



Looking at the temporal movement in disclosure within the banks that eventually become distressed:

Do banks signal their impending distress better in countries with less reporting discretion?

Information content \(\approx\) magnitude of the loading on an accounting variable in a distress-prediction specification that controls for bank-fixed effects

Loading expected to be greater (in absolute magnitude) in countries with more reporting discretion.

$$ Pr(D_{ict} = 1) = \text{Logit}(\alpha_{i} + \theta_1 * x_{ict} + \theta_2 * R_{ct} * x_{ict} + \epsilon_{ict} )$$

Conclusions



Evidence consistent with the idea that reporting discretion diminishes the quality of bank disclosure.

If true, how do banks apply accounting discretion to obfuscate their reported performance?

Part II

LR vs RWCR Debate:

Evidence on the shortcomings of Basel Risk Weights

$$RWCR = \frac{\text{CET1}}{\text{RWA}} = \frac{\frac{CET1}{TA}}{\frac{RWA}{TA}} \approx \frac{LR}{RW}$$

Theoretically (Blume, 2008) and practically, RW more prone to strategic reporting:

  1. F-IRB, A-IRB \(\Longrightarrow\) discretion in setting RW \(\Longrightarrow\) incentive to underweight
  2. Reluctance of existing bank shareholders to issue new equity, especially in times of distress \(\Longrightarrow\) expect less endogeneity in LR


Evidence?

Evidence on strategic disclosure of RW



A: Relative Performance of LR and RW in the prediction of bank distress

Result 1: For a subset of distressed banks, prior to distress: (1) negative trend in LR, (2) negative trend in RW, (3) relatively constant RWCR.

Result 2: LR explains substantially larger fraction of observed distress than RW

(RW does have some, albeit small relative contribution)

B: Time-series behavior of RWCR components at the bank level.

When a bank experiences a negative shock in its equity capital:

Result 3: it tends to decrease its reported RW in positive proportion with the shock

Result 4 tends to allocate larger fraction (in proportion with the shock) of its assets into government bonds
